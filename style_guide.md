# Style Guide

## Aim

Write a harmonized Gospel narrative that reads as though one careful author composed it from Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John.

## Voice

- Use dignified, clear English.
- Keep the tone reverent, calm, and literary.
- Prefer a steady narrative voice over abrupt shifts in diction.
- Avoid colloquial, casual, or highly contemporary phrasing.

## Translation Approach

- Follow a meaning-for-meaning approach when it improves readability.
- Guard every sentence with formal accuracy to the source meaning.
- Do not add ideas, motives, or explanations not present in the biblical text.
- Do not import theological terms or interpretive language unless the text itself supports them.
- Do not closely echo the distinctive wording of any existing English Bible translation.
- If a sentence sounds recognizably tied to a well-known translation, recast it into fresh prose while preserving the original sense.

## Sentence Style

- Favor clear, moderately formal sentences.
- Preserve biblical weight without sounding archaic for its own sake.
- Avoid excessive paraphrase.
- Prefer Scripture-shaped phrasing over explanatory commentary.
- In teaching sections, retain the concrete sayings, images, contrasts, and commands of the text rather than replacing them with abstract explanation.
- When a passage is built as a sequence of blessings, woes, commands, or contrasts, preserve that sequence and cadence where possible instead of turning it into reporter-style summary.
- For major discourses, prophecies, and testimony scenes, prefer expanded harmony that lets the speech stand more directly, even if the section becomes longer.
- Use third-person summary sparingly in speech-heavy passages; if a paragraph still sounds like a report about what was said, it is probably too compressed.
- Avoid devotional or interpretive restatements that sound like commentary on the passage instead of a close retelling of the passage.
- Avoid repetitive sentence openings when merging parallel accounts.
- Prefer concise transitions that feel natural within the story.

## Content Rules

- Keep only one telling of repeated events unless distinct details must be preserved.
- Merge parallel passages without visible seams.
- Preserve meaningful differences in detail when they complement rather than conflict.
- Resolve chronology carefully and consistently across sections.
- Do not force harmonization by inventing missing narrative links.

## Formatting

- Use pure prose in the body text.
- Do not include verse numbers in the prose.
- Begin each section with a title and reference line only.
- Keep notes, mapping, and drafting material outside the narrative body.
- For review drafts, append paragraph-level source references in this form: `<span class="review-ref">[Matt 1:18-25]</span>`.
- Reader editions may hide `.review-ref` markup by stylesheet instead of rewriting the manuscript.

## Preferred Vocabulary

- Choose words that are clear, precise, and scripturally fitting.
- Prefer terms such as `multitude`, `teacher`, `synagogue`, `kingdom`, and `disciples` where context supports them.
- Avoid slang, therapeutic language, and modern idioms.
- Avoid exaggerated archaism such as unnecessary `thee` or `thou`.

## Revision Checklist

- Is the paragraph faithful to the source texts?
- Does it read smoothly as one narrative?
- Has repetition been removed without losing important details?
- Does the tone match the rest of the project?
- Does it sound like a close retelling of Scripture rather than an explanation of Scripture?
- In speech-heavy passages, does it preserve enough of the actual speech to avoid sounding like summary?
- Are references tracked in `references.md`?
