# Reference Mapping

Use this file to track which Gospel passages are included in each section and how overlapping accounts were merged.

## Format

For each section, record:

- Section title
- Passage range by Gospel
- Chronological notes
- Merge decisions
- Open questions for revision

## Sections

### 00. Prologue: The Word Before All Things

- Matthew: none
- Mark: none
- Luke: none
- John: 1:1-18
- Chronology notes: This section stands before the earthly timeline as a theological prologue, introducing the eternal identity of Christ before the narrative begins with events in the days of Herod.
- Merge decisions: Preserved the elevated and declarative tone of John's opening rather than forcing it into scene-based narrative; kept John the Baptist references within the prologue as anticipatory witness material; retained key Johannine phrasing where it carries the theological weight of the passage.
- Open questions: Decide in later revision whether the final line should read "only Son" or "only begotten God/Son" depending on the textual basis preferred for this project; consider whether the prologue should remain slightly more formal than the narrative sections that follow.

### 01. Birth and Early Years

- Matthew: 1:1-25; 2:1-23
- Mark: none
- Luke: 1:5-80; 2:1-52
- John: none
- Chronology notes: Zechariah and Elizabeth precede the annunciation to Mary; Joseph's dream follows Mary's return and explains his acceptance of her pregnancy; the temple presentation is placed after the shepherds and before the visit of the magi; the magi visit the Child in a house, after which the flight to Egypt and later settlement in Nazareth follow; Luke's summary return to Nazareth is read as compressed rather than excluding Matthew's Egypt narrative.
- Merge decisions: Included both annunciations, both infancy witnesses, and both early-childhood episodes without duplicating material; retained direct speech where it carries major theological weight; condensed Mary's song, Simeon's blessing, and the angelic announcements while preserving their central meaning; preserved Matthew's fulfillment material only where it naturally supports the narrative.
- Open questions: Decide whether later revisions should condense direct quotations further for a more uniform narrative texture; confirm whether the genealogy material belongs in a separate appendix or in a future prefatory section rather than the narrative itself.

### 02. John the Baptist and Beginning of Ministry

- Matthew: 3:1-17; 4:1-17
- Mark: 1:1-15
- Luke: 3:1-23; 4:1-15
- John: 1:19-51; 2:1-25; 3:1-36; 4:1-3
- Chronology notes: Began with John's public ministry and witness; placed Jesus' baptism before the wilderness temptation; kept John's post-baptism testimony and the calling of the first disciples after the temptation, following John's narrative sequence; included Cana, the first temple cleansing, Nicodemus, and the Judean baptizing period before the departure from Judea and the summary transition to Galilee after John's imprisonment.
- Merge decisions: Combined the Synoptic preaching summaries with John's formal testimony to the Jerusalem delegation; preserved the three temptation scenes in a familiar sequence while keeping Luke's note that the devil departed until an opportune time; merged the earliest disciple call material from John with the broader opening-of-ministry movement from the Synoptics without yet introducing the later fishing call scenes; restored John 2:23-25 and John 3 as essential ministry-opening material before Samaria and Galilee.
- Open questions: Decide in later revision whether the opening genealogy notice in Luke 3:23 should be reflected in an appendix alongside the Matthew genealogy; confirm whether the first temple cleansing should remain here or be shifted later if the project eventually adopts a single-cleansing harmonization.

### 03. Galilean Ministry

- Matthew: 4:18-18:9
- Mark: 1:16-9:32
- Luke: 4:16-9:45
- John: 4:1-6:71
- Chronology notes: Began with the Samaritan and early Galilean material from John 4 before the settled Capernaum ministry; treated the Nazareth synagogue rejection as an early Galilean episode before the main Capernaum base; merged the calling by the sea with Luke's miraculous catch as one narrative moment; placed John 5 within the Galilean conflict cycle; restored the mission of the Twelve, Herod's reaction, John the Baptist's death, and the late Galilean block leading from the feeding narratives to Peter's confession.
- Merge decisions: Compressed repeated healings and touring summaries while preserving major representative scenes; kept teaching material only where it was inseparable from the surrounding narrative, leaving larger discourse blocks for the next section; combined parallel miracle accounts into single tellings and used John's signs to widen the Galilean arc without interrupting the Synoptic flow; restored the Syrophoenician woman, the deaf man, the feeding of the four thousand, and the blind man at Bethsaida before the confession at Caesarea Philippi.
- Open questions: Decide later how much of John 6 should remain in the narrative section versus being echoed in the teachings section; confirm whether Bethesda is retained here if the project later narrows its chronology more strictly around Judean feasts; review whether the later Nazareth rejection tradition should remain folded into this early phase or receive a separate note.

### 04. Teachings and Parables

- Matthew: 5:1-7:29; 11:25-30; 13:1-52; 18:1-35; 22:15-25:46
- Mark: 4:1-34; 9:33-50; 10:1-31; 12:13-44; 13:1-37
- Luke: 6:17-49; 10:25-37; 11:1-13; 12:13-48; 15:1-32; 16:1-31; 17:1-10; 18:1-17; 20:20-47; 21:5-36
- John: 7:1-52; 8:12-59; 9:1-41; 10:1-42
- Chronology notes: Arranged the teachings in a broadly chronological progression: early Galilean kingdom instruction, parables by the sea, mercy and discipleship teachings, then Jerusalem feast and temple discourses, ending with the late watchfulness and judgment teaching before the passion.
- Merge decisions: Shifted the section away from report-style summary toward expanded harmony, especially in the Sermon material, major parables, and the Jerusalem discourse blocks; merged the Sermon on the Mount and Sermon on the Plain by preserving shared sayings in a single direct-speech movement rather than keeping separate sermon forms; grouped related parables of the kingdom, mercy, prayer, wealth, discipleship, and readiness while preserving more direct speech and more of each discourse unit's inner shape; completed a later consistency pass so the section now matches the expanded-harmony standard used across the manuscript more closely.
- Open questions: Review whether the woman caught in adultery should be addressed in a note or omitted entirely from the harmonized narrative; confirm whether the late temple discourse belongs here or should be partially shifted into the final-journey section; decide in later revision whether some grouped discourse units should be broken into shorter paragraphs for readability without losing the single-flow structure.

### 05. Final Journey to Jerusalem

- Matthew: 19:13-30; 20:17-34; 21:1-22; 26:6-13
- Mark: 10:13-52; 11:1-26; 14:3-9
- Luke: 9:51-10:42; 13:22; 17:11-19; 18:15-19:48
- John: 7:1-13; 10:22-42; 11:1-57; 12:1-50
- Chronology notes: Began with Luke's turning point in 9:51, then wove in the Jerusalem-feast framework from John; placed the Feast of Dedication and the Lazarus narrative before the final Jericho ascent; followed John's timing in placing the Bethany anointing before the triumphal entry while retaining the Synoptic parallels; ended with the close of Jesus' public ministry before the passion proper.
- Merge decisions: Kept the larger discourse blocks in section 4 and used this section mainly for movement, conflict, and arrival scenes; preserved the seventy-two, Martha and Mary, the ten lepers, Lazarus, Jericho, Zacchaeus, Bethany, the triumphal entry, the temple cleansing, the fig tree, and the Greek visitors as the main narrative pillars; used John's chronology to frame the final approach while allowing Luke's travel material and the Synoptic Jericho-to-Jerusalem sequence to flow into it.
- Open questions: Decide in later revision whether the seventy-two mission should remain here or be reduced to a shorter travel notice; confirm whether the Bethany anointing should remain in this section or be moved into the passion prelude if a stricter Synoptic ordering is preferred; review whether the Jerusalem public-ministry close in John 12 should be expanded slightly or left as a brief transition into the passion.

### 06. Crucifixion

- Matthew: 26:1-5, 14-75; 27:1-66
- Mark: 14:1-2, 10-72; 15:1-47
- Luke: 22:1-23:56
- John: 13:1-19:42
- Chronology notes: Began with the plot and Judas's agreement after moving the Bethany anointing into section 5 by Johannine chronology; kept the supper, farewell discourse, and high-priestly prayer within the passion flow; followed the arrest with the Annas-to-Caiaphas sequence, then the Pilate-Herod-Pilate cycle, and continued through crucifixion, burial, and the setting of the guard.
- Merge decisions: Combined the Synoptic institution of bread and cup with John's footwashing and farewell material; preserved the major direct-speech moments at the table, in Gethsemane, at the hearings, and at the cross while still condensing the longest discourse units; integrated the distinctive Lukan and Johannine passion details, including the look to Peter, Herod's hearing, the repentant criminal, the beloved disciple at the cross, the spear thrust, and the sealed tomb.
- Open questions: Decide later whether the farewell discourse in John 14-17 should be expanded further or remain at its present compressed length; review whether the sequence of the Jewish hearing material should receive a brief note for readers because the Gospel presentations differ in emphasis; check whether Pilate's exchanges and the crucifixion sayings should be expanded by one more pass if the later sections prove more speech-rich than this one.

### 07. Resurrection

- Matthew: 28:1-20
- Mark: 16:1-8, 9-11
- Luke: 24:1-53
- John: 20:1-21:25
- Chronology notes: Began with the women at the tomb before separating Mary Magdalene's return to Peter and John from the other women's encounter with the angels and with Jesus; kept the Emmaus appearance and the first Jerusalem appearance on the same day; placed Thomas one week later, then the Galilee sea appearance and Galilee mountain commission, and closed with the later Bethany departure and blessing.
- Merge decisions: Used John's sequence to distinguish Mary's individual encounter from the broader group of women; preserved Luke's Emmaus account and evening Jerusalem appearance alongside John's locked-room details; retained the Galilee manifestations from Matthew and John without forcing them into the same scene; closed with Luke's ascension and John's purpose statement rather than trying to merge all Gospel endings into one final paragraph.
- Open questions: Decide in later revision whether the closing paragraph should end with John's purpose statement, John's final witness note, or Luke's worshipful return to Jerusalem; review whether the brief use of Mark 16:9-11 should remain, since later revision may prefer to avoid any dependence on the longer ending of Mark; consider whether the appearances should eventually be broken into slightly shorter paragraphs for easier reading in print.
