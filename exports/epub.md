# Purpose and Limits

This project seeks to present a readable and faithful harmonized narrative of Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John in one continuous account. Its purpose is to help readers follow the life, ministry, death, and resurrection of Jesus in a single literary flow while remaining as close as possible to the meaning of the biblical text.

Even where this work strives to stay very near the wording and intent of Scripture, it is not itself Scripture. It is not a Bible translation, and it is not meant to replace the Bible. The Bible remains the primary and authoritative text. This volume is only a secondary tool for reading, comparison, reflection, and study.

Readers are encouraged to consult the Gospel texts directly alongside this harmony, especially where chronology, emphasis, and parallel details are being considered.

# Preface

This volume arranges the Gospel material into a single narrative sequence while preserving the distinct details that can stand together without contradiction. Repetition is reduced, parallel scenes are merged, and the wording is recast into a unified voice for continuous reading.

Used well, a harmony like this can help readers follow the full movement of Jesus' life and ministry without repeatedly leaving one account to assemble another. It can make the narrative arc easier to trace, help major speeches and turning points stand in relation to one another, and give a clearer sense of sequence, development, and cumulative force across the four Gospels.

Two presentation modes are anticipated in this project. The review edition retains paragraph-level source references for editorial verification. The reader edition suppresses those references in the visible text so that the work reads more like a continuous book.

The text of this harmony is newly written prose based on the source passages identified in the references. It should not be treated as a substitute for reading the canonical Gospels themselves.

# Prologue: The Word Before All Things

References: John 1:1-18

At the beginning the Word already was. The Word was with God, and the Word was God. He was with God at the beginning. Through Him all things came to be, and apart from Him not one thing came to be. In Him was life, and that life was the light of men. The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it.

There was a man sent from God whose name was John. He came as a witness, to bear witness concerning the Light, so that through him men might believe. He was not the Light, but came to bear witness concerning the Light.

The true Light, which gives light to every man, was coming into the world. He was in the world, and though the world came to be through Him, the world did not know Him. He came to His own, and His own did not receive Him. But to all who did receive Him, to those believing in His name, He gave the right to become children of God, born not of blood, nor of the will of the flesh, nor of the will of man, but of God.

And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we saw His glory, glory as of the Father's only Son, full of grace and truth. John bore witness concerning Him and cried out, "This is the One of whom I said, 'He who comes after me has gone before me, because He was before me.'"

From His fullness we have all received, even grace upon grace. The law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ. No one has ever seen God; the only Son, who is at the Father's side, has made Him known. 

# Birth and Early Years

References: Matthew 1-2; Luke 1-2

In the days of Herod, king of Judea, there was a priest named Zechariah, of the division of Abijah. His wife Elizabeth was from the daughters of Aaron, and they were both righteous before God, walking blamelessly in all the commandments and statutes of the Lord. Yet they had no child, because Elizabeth was barren, and they were both advanced in years.

While Zechariah was carrying out his priestly service before God with his division, the lot fell to him to go into the Lord's sanctuary and offer incense. The people stood outside praying at the time of the offering. Then an angel of the Lord appeared to him on the right side of the altar. Zechariah was shaken by the sight, and fear fell upon him. But the angel said, "Do not fear, Zechariah. Your petition has been heard. Elizabeth your wife will bear you a son, and you are to name him John. He will be great before the Lord, he will turn many in Israel back to their God, and he will go before the Lord in the spirit and power of Elijah to prepare a people made ready for Him."

Zechariah answered, "How can I be certain of this? I am an old man, and my wife too is far along in years." The angel replied, "I am Gabriel, who stands before God. I was sent to speak to you and bring you this good news. Since you did not trust my words, you will be unable to speak until the day these things happen, for they will be fulfilled at the appointed time." When Zechariah came out, he could not speak to the people, and they understood that he had seen a vision in the temple. Afterward Elizabeth conceived, and for five months she kept to herself, saying, "Thus the Lord has dealt with me in the days when He looked on me, to take away my reproach among men."

In Elizabeth's sixth month, God sent Gabriel to Nazareth in Galilee, to a virgin pledged in marriage to a man named Joseph, of David's house. The virgin's name was Mary. The angel came to her and said, "Greetings, you who have found favor. The Lord is with you." She was troubled at the saying and tried to discern what kind of greeting this might be. But the angel said, "Do not fear, Mary, for you have found favor with God. You will conceive and bear a Son, and you are to call His name Jesus. He will be great and will be called the Son of the Most High, and the Lord God will give Him the throne of David His father. He will reign over the house of Jacob forever, and of His kingdom there will be no end."

Mary said to the angel, "How can this be, since I have not known a man?" The angel answered, "The Holy Spirit will come upon you, and the power of the Most High will overshadow you. Therefore the holy Child to be born will be called the Son of God. And behold, Elizabeth your kinswoman has also conceived a son in her old age, and this is the sixth month for her who was called barren. For no word from God will be impossible." Mary said, "Behold, I am the Lord's servant. Let it be to me according to your word." Then the angel departed from her.

In those days Mary arose and went with haste into the hill country, to a town of Judah. She entered the house of Zechariah and greeted Elizabeth. When Elizabeth heard Mary's greeting, the child leaped in her womb, and Elizabeth was filled with the Holy Spirit. She cried out with a loud voice, "Blessed are you among women, and blessed is the fruit of your womb. Why has this happened to me, that the mother of my Lord should come to me? For behold, when the sound of your greeting came to my ears, the child in my womb leaped for joy. Blessed is she who believed that what was spoken to her from the Lord would be fulfilled." Then Mary said, "My soul magnifies the Lord, and my spirit rejoices in God my Savior, because He has looked upon the low estate of His servant. From now on all generations will call me blessed, because the Mighty One has done great things for me, and holy is His name. His mercy is from generation to generation toward those who fear Him. He has shown strength with His arm, scattered the proud in the imagination of their hearts, brought down rulers from their thrones, and lifted up the lowly. He has filled the hungry with good things, and the rich He has sent away empty. He has helped Israel His servant, remembering mercy, as He spoke to our fathers, to Abraham and to his offspring forever." She remained with Elizabeth about three months and then returned to her home.

The birth of Jesus the Messiah came about in this way. Mary had been pledged to Joseph, but before they came together she was found to be carrying a child by the Holy Spirit. Joseph, being a righteous man and unwilling to expose her publicly, resolved to dismiss her quietly. But while he considered this, an angel of the Lord appeared to him in a dream and said, "Joseph, son of David, do not hesitate to take Mary as your wife, because the Child conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit. She will bear a Son, and you are to name Him Jesus, for He will save His people from their sins." All this happened to fulfill what the Lord had spoken through the prophet: the virgin would conceive and bear a son, and He would be called Immanuel, meaning God with us. When Joseph awoke, he did what the angel of the Lord had directed and took Mary as his wife. He did not know her as a husband does until she had given birth to a son, and he named Him Jesus.

When the time came for Elizabeth to give birth, she bore a son. On the eighth day they came to circumcise the child, and they would have called him Zechariah after his father. But his mother answered, "No, he shall be called John." They said to her, "There is no one among your relatives who is called by this name," so they made signs to his father to learn what he wanted him called. Zechariah asked for a tablet and wrote, "His name is John." Immediately his mouth was opened and his tongue loosed, and he spoke, blessing God. Fear came on all who lived around them, and all these matters were talked about throughout the hill country of Judea.

Then Zechariah, filled with the Holy Spirit, prophesied and said, "Blessed be the Lord, the God of Israel, because He has visited His people and worked redemption for them. He has raised up a horn of salvation for us in the house of David His servant, just as He spoke by the mouth of His holy prophets from of old: salvation from our enemies and from the hand of all who hate us. He has shown mercy promised to our fathers and remembered His holy covenant, the oath He swore to Abraham our father, to grant that we, being delivered from the hand of our enemies, might serve Him without fear, in holiness and righteousness before Him all our days. And you, child, will be called prophet of the Most High, for you will go before the Lord to prepare His ways, to give His people the knowledge of salvation through the forgiveness of their sins, because of the tender mercy of our God. By this the dawn from on high will visit us, to shine upon those who sit in darkness and in the shadow of death, and to guide our feet into the way of peace." And the child grew and became strong in spirit, and he remained in the wilderness until the day of his appearing to Israel.

In those days a decree went out from Caesar Augustus that all the world should be registered. So Joseph also went up from Galilee, from the city of Nazareth, to Judea, to the city of David, which is called Bethlehem, because he was of the house and lineage of David, to be registered with Mary, who was betrothed to him and was with child. While they were there, the days were fulfilled for her to give birth. She gave birth to her firstborn Son, wrapped Him in swaddling cloths, and laid Him in a manger, because there was no place for them in the guest room.

In that same region shepherds were out in the fields at night, keeping watch over their flock. Suddenly an angel of the Lord stood before them, and the glory of the Lord shone around them, so that they were greatly afraid. But the angel said, "Do not fear. Behold, I bring you good news of great joy that will be for all the people. Today, in David's city, a Savior has been born for you, who is Christ the Lord. This will be the sign for you: you will find a baby wrapped in swaddling cloths and lying in a manger." And suddenly there was with the angel a multitude of the heavenly host, praising God and saying, "Glory to God in the highest, and on earth peace among men on whom His favor rests." When the angels had gone away into heaven, the shepherds said to one another, "Let us go now to Bethlehem and see this thing that has happened, which the Lord has made known to us." They went with haste and found Mary and Joseph, and the baby lying in the manger. Afterward they made known what had been spoken to them concerning the Child, and all who heard it marveled at what the shepherds said. But Mary treasured up all these things and pondered them in her heart. Then the shepherds returned, glorifying and praising God for all they had heard and seen, just as it had been told them.

When eight days were completed for His circumcision, He was named Jesus, the name given by the angel before He was conceived in the womb. And when the days of their purification according to the Law of Moses were finished, they brought Him up to Jerusalem to present Him to the Lord and to offer the sacrifice appointed in the Law of the Lord.

At that time there was a man in Jerusalem named Simeon, righteous and devout, waiting for the consolation of Israel, and the Holy Spirit was upon him. The Spirit had revealed to him that he would not see death before he had seen the Lord's Christ. Led by the Spirit, he came into the temple courts; and when the parents brought in the Child Jesus to do for Him according to the custom of the Law, Simeon took Him in his arms and blessed God, saying, "Now, Master, You are letting Your servant depart in peace according to Your word, because my eyes have seen Your salvation, which You prepared in the sight of all peoples, a light for revelation to the nations and the glory of Your people Israel." Joseph and His mother marveled at what was spoken concerning Him. Then Simeon blessed them and said to Mary His mother, "Behold, this Child is set for the falling and rising of many in Israel, and for a sign that will be spoken against, so that the thoughts of many hearts may be revealed. And a sword will pass through your own soul also."

There was also a prophetess, Anna, the daughter of Phanuel, of the tribe of Asher. She did not depart from the temple, worshiping with fasting and prayer night and day. Coming up at that very hour, she gave thanks to God and spoke of the Child to all who were waiting for the redemption of Jerusalem.

After Jesus was born in Bethlehem of Judea in the days of Herod the king, magi from the east arrived in Jerusalem, saying, "Where is He who has been born King of the Jews? For we saw His star in the east and have come to worship Him." When Herod heard this, he was troubled, and all Jerusalem with him. Gathering together the chief priests and scribes of the people, he inquired of them where the Christ was to be born. They said to him, "In Bethlehem of Judea," for so it had been written by the prophet.

Then Herod secretly called the magi and learned from them the exact time the star had appeared. He sent them to Bethlehem and said, "Go and search carefully for the Child. And when you have found Him, report to me, so that I too may come and worship Him." After hearing the king, they went their way. And behold, the star they had seen went before them until it came and stood over the place where the Child was. When they saw the star, they rejoiced exceedingly with great joy. Entering the house, they saw the Child with Mary His mother, and they fell down and worshiped Him. Then they opened their treasures and presented to Him gifts of gold, frankincense, and myrrh. And having been warned in a dream not to return to Herod, they departed to their own country by another way.

When they had departed, an angel of the Lord appeared to Joseph in a dream and said, "Rise, take the Child and His mother, and flee to Egypt. Remain there until I tell you, for Herod is going to search for the Child to destroy Him." So Joseph rose, took the Child and His mother by night, and departed for Egypt, and remained there until the death of Herod. Then Herod, when he saw that he had been deceived by the magi, became furious and sent and killed all the boys in Bethlehem and in all its region who were two years old and under, according to the time he had learned from the magi.

When Herod died, an angel of the Lord appeared in a dream to Joseph in Egypt and said, "Rise, take the Child and His mother, and go to the land of Israel, for those who sought the Child's life are dead." So he rose and took the Child and His mother and came into the land of Israel. But when he heard that Archelaus was reigning over Judea in place of his father Herod, he was afraid to go there. Being warned in a dream, he withdrew to the district of Galilee and came to live in a city called Nazareth. And the Child grew and became strong, filled with wisdom, and the favor of God was upon Him.

Now His parents went to Jerusalem every year at the Feast of the Passover. And when He was twelve years old, they went up according to the custom of the feast. After the feast was over, while they were returning, the boy Jesus stayed behind in Jerusalem, but His parents were unaware of it. Supposing Him to be in the company, they traveled a day's journey, and then they began searching for Him among their relatives and acquaintances. When they did not find Him, they returned to Jerusalem looking for Him. After three days they found Him in the temple, sitting among the teachers, listening to them and asking them questions. All who heard Him were astonished at His understanding and His answers. When His parents saw Him, they were amazed. His mother said to Him, "Son, why have You treated us this way? Your father and I have been anxiously searching for You." He said to them, "Why were you searching for Me? Did you not know that I had to be in My Father's house?" Yet they did not understand the statement He spoke to them.

Then He went down with them and came to Nazareth, and He continued in submission to them. His mother treasured all these things in her heart. And Jesus increased in wisdom and stature, and in favor with God and men. 

# John the Baptist and Beginning of Ministry

References: Matthew 3-4; Mark 1; Luke 3-4; John 1-4

In the fifteenth year of Tiberius Caesar, when Pontius Pilate governed Judea, Herod ruled Galilee, Philip ruled Iturea and Trachonitis, and Lysanias ruled Abilene, during the high priesthood of Annas and Caiaphas, the word of God came to John son of Zechariah in the wilderness. He went through the whole region around the Jordan, proclaiming a baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins, according to what was written in Isaiah the prophet about a voice crying in the wilderness to prepare the way of the Lord and make His paths straight.

John wore clothing of camel's hair and a leather belt around his waist, and his food was locusts and wild honey. Jerusalem and all Judea, and all the district around the Jordan, were going out to him. They were baptized by him in the Jordan, confessing their sins. But when he saw many of the Pharisees and Sadducees coming for baptism, he said, "Brood of vipers, who warned you to flee from the wrath that is coming? Bear fruit worthy of repentance, and do not begin to say within yourselves, 'We have Abraham as our father.' God is able from these stones to raise up children for Abraham. Already the axe lies at the root of the trees. Every tree that does not bear good fruit is cut down and thrown into the fire."

The people asked him, "What then should we do?" He answered, "Whoever has two tunics must share with the one who has none, and whoever has food must do likewise." Tax collectors also came and asked, "Teacher, what should we do?" He said, "Take no more than has been appointed." Soldiers also asked, "And we, what should we do?" He answered, "Do not extort, do not accuse falsely, and be content with your pay." Since the people were in expectation and all were wondering in their hearts whether John might be the Christ, he answered them all, saying, "I baptize you with water, but One stronger than I is coming, and I am not worthy even to loosen the strap of His sandals. He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and with fire. His winnowing fork is in His hand to clear His threshing floor, gather the wheat into His barn, and burn the chaff with unquenchable fire." So John exhorted the people in many ways and preached good news to them.

This is the witness John gave when the Jews sent priests and Levites from Jerusalem to ask who he was. He confessed openly, "I am not the Christ." When they asked whether he was Elijah, he said, "I am not." When they asked whether he was the Prophet, he answered, "No." Then he said, "I am the voice of one crying in the wilderness, 'Make straight the way of the Lord.'" When they asked why he baptized if he was none of these, John answered, "I baptize with water, but One already stands among you whom you do not know, the One coming after me, whose sandal I am not worthy even to loosen."

When the people were receiving baptism, Jesus also came from Nazareth in Galilee to the Jordan to be baptized by John. John tried to prevent Him, saying, "I need to be baptized by You, and do You come to me?" But Jesus answered, "Permit it now, for in this way it is fitting for us to fulfill all righteousness." Then John consented. After Jesus was baptized and had come up from the water praying, heaven was opened. The Spirit of God came down upon Him like a dove, and a voice came from heaven, saying, "This is My beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased." Jesus was about thirty years old when He began.

John also bore witness, saying, "I saw the Spirit descend from heaven like a dove and remain on Him. I myself did not know Him, but the One who sent me to baptize with water told me, 'The Man on whom you see the Spirit descend and remain, this is the One who baptizes with the Holy Spirit.' I have seen and have borne witness that this is the Son of God."

At once the Spirit led Jesus out into the wilderness, where for forty days He was tempted by the devil. He ate nothing through those days, and when they ended, He was hungry. The tempter said to Him, "If You are the Son of God, command these stones to become bread." Jesus answered, "Man shall not live by bread alone, but by every word that comes from God." Then the devil took Him to the holy city and set Him on the pinnacle of the temple, saying, "If You are the Son of God, throw Yourself down, for it is written that He will command His angels concerning You, and that they will bear You up." Jesus replied, "It is also written: 'You shall not put the Lord your God to the test.'" Again the devil showed Him all the kingdoms of the world and their glory in a moment, and said, "All these I will give You if You will fall down and worship me." But Jesus said to him, "Go, Satan, for it is written: 'You shall worship the Lord your God, and Him only shall you serve.'" After every temptation had ended, the devil left Him for a time, and angels came and ministered to Him.

The next day John saw Jesus coming toward him and said, "Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world. This is the One of whom I said, 'After me comes a Man who has gone before me, because He was before me.'" The following day John was standing with two of his disciples, and when he saw Jesus walking by, he again said, "Behold, the Lamb of God." The two disciples heard this and followed Jesus. One of them was Andrew, Simon Peter's brother. He first found Simon and told him, "We have found the Messiah," that is, the Christ, and he brought him to Jesus. Jesus looked at him and said, "You are Simon son of John. You will be called Cephas," which means Peter.

The next day Jesus decided to leave for Galilee. He found Philip and said, "Follow Me." Philip then found Nathanael and said, "We have found the One written about by Moses in the Law and by the prophets: Jesus of Nazareth, Joseph's son." Nathanael asked, "Can anything good come out of Nazareth?" Philip said, "Come and see." When Jesus saw Nathanael approaching, He said, "Behold, a true Israelite in whom there is no deceit." Nathanael asked, "How do You know me?" Jesus answered, "Before Philip called you, when you were under the fig tree, I saw you." Nathanael answered, "Rabbi, You are the Son of God. You are the King of Israel." Jesus said, "You will see greater things than these. You will see heaven opened and the angels of God ascending and descending upon the Son of Man."

On the third day there was a wedding at Cana in Galilee, and Jesus' mother was there. Jesus and His disciples were also invited. When the wine was gone, His mother said to Him, "They have no wine." Jesus said to her, "Woman, why do you bring this to Me? My hour has not yet come." His mother said to the servants, "Do whatever He tells you." Then He told the servants to fill the stone jars with water. After they had filled them completely, He said, "Draw some out now and take it to the master of the feast." When the master tasted it, the water had become wine, though only the servants knew where it had come from. He called the bridegroom and said, "Every man sets out the good wine first, and afterward the poorer. But you have kept the good wine until now." By this first sign at Cana, Jesus manifested His glory, and His disciples believed in Him.

After this He went down to Capernaum with His mother, His brothers, and His disciples, and they remained there a few days. When the Passover approached, Jesus went up to Jerusalem. In the temple courts He found men selling oxen, sheep, and doves, and money changers seated at their tables. Making a whip from cords, He drove them all out with the sheep and cattle, scattered the money changers' coins, and overturned their tables. He told those selling doves, "Take these things away. Do not make My Father's house a house of trade." His disciples remembered the Scripture that says zeal for God's house would consume Him.

The Jewish leaders then asked, "What sign do You show us, seeing that You do these things?" Jesus answered, "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up." They replied, "This temple has been forty-six years in building, and will You raise it up in three days?" But He was speaking about the temple of His own body. So after He rose from the dead, His disciples remembered that He had said this and believed both the Scripture and His word.

While He was in Jerusalem at the Passover feast, many believed in His name when they saw the signs He was doing. But Jesus did not entrust Himself to them, because He knew all men and had no need for anyone to testify concerning man, for He knew what was in man.

There was a Pharisee named Nicodemus, one of the Jewish rulers, who came to Jesus by night and said, "Rabbi, we know that You have come from God as a teacher, because no one could do these signs unless God were with him." Jesus answered him, "I tell you truly, unless a man is born from above, he cannot see the kingdom of God." Nicodemus asked, "How can a man be born when he is old? Can he enter his mother's womb a second time and be born?" Jesus answered, "Unless a man is born of water and Spirit, he cannot enter the kingdom of God. What is born of flesh is flesh, and what is born of the Spirit is spirit. Do not marvel that I said to you, 'You must be born from above.' The wind blows where it wills, and you hear its sound, but you do not know from where it comes or where it goes. So it is with everyone born of the Spirit." Nicodemus said, "How can these things be?" Jesus answered, "Are you the teacher of Israel and yet you do not understand these things? As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so the Son of Man must be lifted up, so that everyone who believes in Him may have eternal life. For God loved the world in this way: He gave His only Son, so that everyone who believes in Him should not perish but have eternal life. God did not send His Son into the world to condemn the world, but that the world might be saved through Him. But the Light has come into the world, and men loved the darkness rather than the Light, because their deeds were evil."

After this Jesus and His disciples went out into the Judean countryside and spent time there, and His disciples were baptizing. John too was baptizing at Aenon near Salim, because there was much water there. When a dispute arose about purification, John's disciples came and said, "Rabbi, the One who was with you beyond the Jordan, to whom you have borne witness, behold, He is baptizing, and all are going to Him." John answered, "A man receives nothing unless it has been given him from heaven. You yourselves can testify that I said, 'I am not the Christ, but I have been sent before Him.' The one who has the bride is the bridegroom. But the friend of the bridegroom stands and hears him, and rejoices greatly because of the bridegroom's voice. So this joy of mine is now full. He must increase, but I must decrease. The One who comes from above is above all. Whoever believes in the Son has eternal life."

When Jesus learned that the Pharisees had heard He was gathering and baptizing more disciples than John, though Jesus Himself did not baptize, but His disciples did, He left Judea. And after Herod had imprisoned John for his rebukes, Jesus returned to Galilee in the Spirit's power, and word about Him spread through the surrounding country. From then on He proclaimed, "The time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of God has drawn near. Repent and believe the good news." 

# Galilean Ministry

References: Matthew 4-18; Mark 1-9; Luke 4-9; John 4-6

As He went northward, Jesus had to pass through Samaria, and He came to a town called Sychar, near the plot of ground Jacob had given to his son Joseph. Wearied from the journey, He sat by the well about the sixth hour while His disciples had gone into the town to buy food. A Samaritan woman came to draw water, and Jesus said to her, "Give Me a drink." The woman said, "How is it that You, being a Jew, ask a drink from me, a Samaritan woman?" Jesus answered, "If you knew the gift of God, and who it is that says to you, 'Give Me a drink,' you would have asked Him, and He would have given you living water."

The woman said, "Sir, You have nothing to draw with and the well is deep. From where, then, do You get this living water? Are You greater than our father Jacob, who gave us the well and drank from it himself?" Jesus answered, "Everyone who drinks of this water will thirst again, but whoever drinks of the water I give him will never thirst. The water I give will become in him a spring of water rising up to eternal life." The woman said, "Sir, give me this water, so that I may not thirst or keep coming here to draw."

Jesus said to her, "Go, call your husband, and come here." The woman answered, "I have no husband." Jesus said, "You have spoken rightly, for you have had five husbands, and the man you now have is not your husband." The woman said, "Sir, I see that You are a prophet. Our fathers worshiped on this mountain, but you Jews say that Jerusalem is the place where men ought to worship." Jesus answered, "Believe Me, woman, the hour is coming when you will worship the Father neither on this mountain nor in Jerusalem. The hour is coming, and now is, when the true worshipers will worship the Father in spirit and truth, for the Father seeks such people to worship Him."

The woman said, "I know that Messiah is coming, and when He comes, He will tell us all things." Jesus said to her, "I who speak to you am He." At that moment His disciples returned and marveled that He was speaking with a woman, yet no one said what he sought or why He was speaking with her. Then the woman left her water jar, went back into the town, and said to the people, "Come, see a Man who told me all that I ever did. Could this be the Christ?" And they went out of the town and were coming to Him.

Meanwhile the disciples urged Him to eat, but He said, "I have food to eat that you do not know about." When they wondered whether someone had brought Him food, Jesus said, "My food is to do the will of the One who sent Me and to finish His work. Lift up your eyes and look at the fields, for they are already white for harvest. The reaper is already receiving wages and gathering fruit for eternal life, so that sower and reaper may rejoice together."

Many Samaritans from that town believed in Him because of the woman's testimony, that He had told her all that she had ever done. So when the Samaritans came to Him, they asked Him to stay with them, and He stayed there two days. And many more believed because of His word. They said to the woman, "It is no longer because of your speaking that we believe, for we have heard for ourselves and know that this One truly is the Savior of the world."

After that He went into Galilee, teaching in their synagogues, and He was glorified by all. He came again to Cana, where He had made the water wine, and there He healed the son of a royal official at Capernaum, speaking the word from a distance and causing the child to live at that very hour. From there He went to Nazareth, where He had been brought up, and as was His custom He entered the synagogue on the Sabbath and stood up to read. The scroll of Isaiah was handed to Him, and He found the place where it was written, "The Spirit of the Lord is upon Me, because He has anointed Me to proclaim good news to the poor. He has sent Me to proclaim liberty to the captives, recovery of sight to the blind, to send the oppressed away in freedom, and to proclaim the year of the Lord's favor." Then He rolled up the scroll, gave it back to the attendant, and sat down. The eyes of all in the synagogue were fixed on Him, and He began to say, "Today this Scripture has been fulfilled in your hearing."

At first all spoke well of Him and marveled at the gracious words that came from His mouth. But He said, "Truly I tell you, no prophet is accepted in his own country. There were many widows in Israel in the days of Elijah, yet Elijah was sent to none of them, but to a widow in Sidon. There were many lepers in Israel in the time of Elisha, yet none of them was cleansed, but only Naaman the Syrian." At this those in the synagogue were filled with wrath. They rose up, drove Him out of the town, and led Him to the brow of the hill in order to throw Him down. But He passed through their midst and went on His way.

Then He came and settled in Capernaum by the sea, in the region of Zebulun and Naphtali, and from that time He continued preaching, "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand." Walking by the Sea of Galilee, He saw Simon and Andrew casting a net into the sea, for they were fishermen. He said to them, "Follow Me, and I will make you fishers of men." They left their nets and followed Him. Going on from there, He saw James and John, the sons of Zebedee, with their father in the boat mending their nets, and He called them also. They immediately left the boat and their father and followed Him.

Once, while the multitude pressed upon Him to hear the word of God, He saw two boats by the lake, and the fishermen had gone out of them and were washing their nets. Getting into Simon's boat, He asked him to put out a little from the land, and He sat down and taught the multitude from the boat. When He had finished speaking, He told Simon, "Put out into deep water, and let down your nets for a catch." Simon answered, "Master, we have toiled all night and taken nothing, but at Your word I will let down the nets." When they had done so, they enclosed such a great number of fish that their nets began to break. Simon Peter fell down at Jesus' knees and said, "Depart from me, Lord, for I am a sinful man." But Jesus said to Simon, "Do not be afraid; from now on you will catch men." So they brought the boats to land, left everything, and followed Him.

He entered Capernaum, and on the Sabbath He taught in the synagogue. The people were astonished at His teaching, for His word carried authority. In the synagogue there was a man with an unclean spirit, and Jesus rebuked the spirit, and it came out of the man without harming him. Amazement came upon them all, and news about Him spread into every place in the surrounding region. Leaving the synagogue, He entered Simon's house and rebuked Simon's mother-in-law's fever, and it left her. At sunset they brought to Him all who were sick or oppressed by demons, and He laid His hands on many and healed them. Very early the next morning He departed to a desolate place, but when the crowds sought Him, He said, "I must preach the good news of the kingdom of God to the other towns also, for that is why I was sent."

A leper came to Him, falling on his knees and saying, "Lord, if You are willing, You can make me clean." Moved with compassion, Jesus stretched out His hand, touched him, and said, "I am willing; be cleansed." Immediately the leprosy left him. Yet the report about Him spread all the more, and great multitudes came to hear Him and to be healed of their infirmities.

When He returned to Capernaum after some days, it was reported that He was at home, and so many gathered that there was no more room, not even at the door. Then men came bringing a paralytic, carried by four of them, and when they could not reach Jesus because of the crowd, they uncovered the roof above Him and lowered the mat on which the paralytic lay. Seeing their faith, Jesus said to the man, "Son, your sins are forgiven." Some of the scribes reasoned in their hearts that He was blaspheming, for who can forgive sins but God alone. Jesus, knowing in His spirit what they were thinking, asked whether it was easier to say, "Your sins are forgiven," or, "Rise, take up your mat and walk." Then He told the paralytic to rise, take up his mat, and go to his house. Immediately he rose and went out before them all, and they glorified God.

After this Jesus saw a tax collector named Levi, called Matthew, sitting at the tax booth, and He said to him, "Follow Me." Leaving everything, he rose and followed Him. Levi made a great feast for Him in his house, and many tax collectors and others were reclining at table with them. When the Pharisees and their scribes complained that He ate with tax collectors and sinners, Jesus answered, "Those who are well have no need of a physician, but those who are sick. I have not come to call the righteous, but sinners, to repentance."

At another time He healed a man at the pool of Bethesda who had been afflicted for many years, saying to him, "Rise, take up your mat, and walk." Because this was done on the Sabbath, the Jews persecuted Him all the more. He also defended His disciples when they plucked heads of grain on the Sabbath, and He healed a man with a withered hand in the synagogue, saying, "The Son of Man is Lord even of the Sabbath," and asking whether it is lawful on the Sabbath to do good or to do harm, to save life or to kill. From that time the Pharisees watched Him with growing hatred and took counsel against Him.

Great multitudes from Galilee, Judea, Jerusalem, Idumea, beyond the Jordan, and the region of Tyre and Sidon came to Him. He withdrew often to the mountain to pray, and from among His disciples He chose twelve, whom He named apostles: Simon, whom He called Peter, and Andrew his brother; James and John; Philip and Bartholomew; Matthew and Thomas; James the son of Alphaeus and Simon called the Zealot; Judas the son of James; and Judas Iscariot, who became a traitor.

He continued teaching in the synagogues, proclaiming the kingdom, and healing every disease and sickness among the people. In Capernaum He marveled at a centurion's faith and healed his servant from afar. At Nain He met a funeral procession for the only son of a widow, and He touched the bier and commanded the young man to arise, and He gave him back to his mother. Women who had been healed of evil spirits and infirmities also followed Him and ministered to Him out of their means.

When John heard in prison about the works of the Christ, he sent word asking whether Jesus was the One who was to come, or whether they should look for another. Jesus said, "Go and tell John what you have seen and heard: the blind receive sight, the lame walk, lepers are cleansed, the deaf hear, the dead are raised, and the poor have good news preached to them. Blessed is the one who does not stumble because of Me." Then He bore witness to John before the people, saying, "What did you go out into the wilderness to see? A reed shaken by the wind? Among those born of women there has not arisen a greater than John the Baptist."

Crowds gathered around Him beside the sea and in the villages, and He spoke many things to them in parables. Yet He also took His disciples aside and taught them privately. He calmed a great storm on the sea with a word, so that those in the boat marveled that even the winds and the waves obeyed Him. On the other side He delivered a man from a legion of demons, and the man who had been possessed proclaimed throughout the Decapolis what great things Jesus had done for him.

When He returned, Jairus, a ruler of the synagogue, fell at His feet and pleaded for his little daughter, who was dying. As Jesus went, a woman who had suffered from a flow of blood for twelve years touched the fringe of His garment, believing she would be healed, and immediately she was made well. Then Jesus went to Jairus's house, took the child by the hand, and raised her up. In many places He healed the blind, the mute, and those brought in weakness to Him, and the crowds marveled, saying, "Nothing like this has ever been seen in Israel."

Though He did many mighty works, not all believed. Some accused Him of casting out demons by the ruler of demons, but He answered, "Every kingdom divided against itself is brought to ruin. If Satan is divided against himself, how will his kingdom stand?" He warned them against speaking against the Holy Spirit. Others sought a sign from heaven, but He said, "No sign will be given to this generation except the sign of Jonah." Even in His own region many stumbled at Him, and because of their unbelief He did not do many mighty works there.

Then He called the Twelve to Himself and began to send them out, giving them authority over unclean spirits and power to preach the kingdom of God, heal the sick, and call men to repentance. He charged them, "Take nothing for the journey beyond what is needed, and wherever you are received, remain there until you depart. Freely you have received; freely give."

When Herod heard of all that was happening, he was perplexed, and some were saying, "John the Baptist has risen from the dead." For Herod had arrested John and bound him because of Herodias, his brother's wife, for John had said to him, "It is not lawful for you to have her." On Herod's birthday the daughter of Herodias danced before the guests and pleased him, and at her mother's urging she asked for the head of John the Baptist on a platter. Though the king was distressed, because of his oaths and his dinner guests he commanded it to be given her. John was beheaded in the prison, and his disciples came, took away the body, buried it, and brought Jesus the news.

When the apostles returned from preaching, Jesus took them aside to a solitary place near Bethsaida. But the crowds learned of it and followed Him on foot from the towns. He welcomed them, spoke to them concerning the kingdom of God, and healed those who needed healing. As evening approached, His disciples urged Him to send the crowd away, but Jesus said, "You give them something to eat." Taking the five loaves and two fish, He looked up to heaven, blessed and broke them, and gave them to the disciples to set before the multitude. All ate and were satisfied, and twelve baskets full of broken pieces were taken up.

When the people saw the sign He had performed, they said, "This is truly the Prophet who is to come into the world." Jesus, knowing they meant to seize Him and make Him king by force, withdrew alone to the mountain. That evening His disciples went down to the sea and set out in the boat. In the last watch of the night Jesus came toward them, walking on the water. They were frightened, but He said, "Take courage. It is I. Do not be afraid." Peter answered, "Lord, if it is You, command me to come to You on the water." Jesus said, "Come." But when Peter saw the wind, fear took hold of him and he began to sink, and Jesus reached out and took hold of him. When they entered the boat, it came at once to the shore, and those with Him worshiped Him, saying, "Truly You are the Son of God."

The next day the crowds searched for Him because they had eaten the loaves and been filled. Jesus said to them, "Do not labor for the food that perishes, but for the food that endures to eternal life, which the Son of Man will give you." They said to Him, "Sir, always give us this bread." Jesus said, "I am the bread of life. Whoever comes to Me will not hunger, and whoever believes in Me will never thirst. The bread of God is the One who comes down from heaven and gives life to the world, and the bread that I will give for the life of the world is My flesh." When many murmured at this, they said, "This word is hard. Who can hear it?" Jesus did not draw back from it. So many of His disciples turned away and no longer walked with Him. Then Jesus said to the Twelve, "Do you also wish to go away?" Simon Peter answered, "Lord, to whom would we go? You have the words of eternal life, and we have believed and come to know that You are the Holy One of God."

After this the Pharisees and scribes from Jerusalem found fault with His disciples for eating with defiled hands, but Jesus answered, "A man is defiled not by what enters from outside, but by what proceeds from the heart." Then He withdrew to the region of Tyre and Sidon. There a Canaanite woman, a Syrophoenician by birth, cried out, "Have mercy on me, Lord, Son of David. My daughter is grievously tormented by a demon." Though He tested her faith and said, "It is not right to take the children's bread and throw it to the dogs," she answered, "Yes, Lord, yet even the little dogs eat the crumbs that fall from their masters' table." Then Jesus said, "O woman, great is your faith. Let it be done for you as you desire," and her daughter was healed from that hour.

Returning by way of Sidon to the Sea of Galilee, through the Decapolis, Jesus healed a man who was deaf and had difficulty speaking, and the people marveled, saying, "He has done all things well. He makes even the deaf hear and the mute speak." Again a large crowd stayed with Him for three days, and He fed about four thousand men, besides women and children, with seven loaves and a few small fish. Afterward the Pharisees demanded a sign from heaven, but Jesus said to His disciples, "Beware the leaven of the Pharisees and Sadducees." Then at Bethsaida He took a blind man by the hand and restored his sight.

Later, when Jesus was praying alone with His disciples nearby, He asked, "Who do the crowds say that I am?" They answered, "Some say John the Baptist, others Elijah, and others one of the prophets." Then He asked, "But who do you yourselves say that I am?" Simon Peter answered, "You are the Christ of God, the Son of the living God." Jesus declared him blessed for that confession and from that time began to teach His disciples openly, saying, "The Son of Man must suffer many things, be rejected by the elders, chief priests, and scribes, be killed, and on the third day rise." He also said, "If anyone wishes to come after Me, let him deny himself, take up his cross each day, and follow Me."

About eight days after these sayings, Jesus took Peter, John, and James up the mountain to pray. While He was praying, the appearance of His face was altered, and His clothing became white and dazzling. Moses and Elijah appeared in glory and spoke with Him about the departure He was soon to accomplish at Jerusalem. Then a bright cloud overshadowed them, and a voice came from the cloud, saying, "This is My Son, whom I have chosen. Hear Him." At this the disciples fell on their faces in fear. Jesus came to them, touched them, and told them to rise and not be afraid. As they came down the mountain, He instructed them to tell no one what they had seen until the Son of Man had risen from the dead.

When they came down to the crowd, a man cried out concerning his son, who was tormented by an unclean spirit. The disciples had been unable to cast it out, but Jesus rebuked the spirit and healed the boy. All were astonished at the majesty of God, yet the disciples did not understand when He again told them, "The Son of Man is about to be delivered into the hands of men."

When He saw the crowds, He went up the mountain, and after He sat down His disciples came to Him. Then He taught them, saying, "Blessed are the poor in spirit, because the kingdom of heaven is theirs. Blessed are those who mourn, because they will be comforted. Blessed are the meek, because they will inherit the earth. Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, because they will be filled. Blessed are the merciful, because they will receive mercy. Blessed are the pure in heart, because they will see God. Blessed are the peacemakers, because they will be called sons of God. Blessed are those who are persecuted because of righteousness, because the kingdom of heaven is theirs. Blessed are you when men hate you, revile you, and speak every false evil against you on My account. Rejoice and be glad in that day, because your reward is great in heaven. But woe to you who are rich now, because you have already received your comfort. Woe to you who are full now, because you will hunger. Woe to you who laugh now, because you will mourn and weep.

You are the salt of the earth. But if salt becomes tasteless, how will its savor be restored? You are the light of the world. A city on a hill does not stay hidden, and a lamp is not lit in order to be put under a basket, but on a stand, so that it gives light to all in the house. Let your light shine before men, so that they may see your good works and glorify your Father in heaven. Do not think that I came to abolish the Law or the Prophets. I did not come to abolish them, but to fulfill them. Unless your righteousness goes beyond that of the scribes and Pharisees, you will not enter the kingdom of heaven."

He continued, "You have heard that it was said to those of old, 'You shall not murder,' and that the murderer is liable to judgment. But I tell you that everyone who remains angry with his brother will answer for it. If you are bringing your gift to the altar and there remember that your brother has something against you, leave your gift there, first go and be reconciled to your brother, and then come and offer your gift.

You have heard that it was said, 'You shall not commit adultery.' But I tell you that whoever looks with desire has already committed adultery in his heart."

He also said, "Again, you have heard that it was said, 'You shall not swear falsely.' But I tell you, do not swear by heaven or by earth. Let what you say be yes or no.

You have heard, 'Eye for eye and tooth for tooth.' But I tell you, do not return evil in kind. If anyone strikes you on one cheek, offer the other also. If anyone compels you to go one mile, go with him two. Give to the one who asks of you, and do not turn away from the one who would borrow from you.

You have heard, 'You shall love your neighbor.' But I tell you, love your enemies, do good to those who hate you, bless those who curse you, and pray for those who mistreat you, so that you may be sons of your Father, who causes His sun to rise on evil and good and sends rain on righteous and unrighteous alike."

He also said, "When you give, do not do it before men in order to be seen by them. When you pray, do not stand to draw notice, but pray to your Father who is unseen. When you fast, do not put on a gloomy face so that men will notice it. The Father sees in secret.

Pray, then, in this way: 'Our Father in heaven, let Your name be hallowed. Let Your kingdom come. Let Your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven. Give us today the bread for this day. Forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors. Do not lead us into trial, but deliver us from evil.'

Do not gather treasure for yourselves on earth, where moth and rust ruin and where thieves break in and steal. Gather treasure in heaven. Where your treasure is, there your heart will also be. The eye is the lamp of the body. No servant can serve two masters. Therefore do not be anxious about your life, what you will eat or drink, or about your body, what you will wear. Your Father knows that you need all these things. Seek first the kingdom of God and His righteousness, and these things will be given to you as well."

He said further, "Do not judge, so that you may not be judged. With the measure you use, it will be measured back to you. Can the blind guide the blind? Will they not both fall into a pit? Why do you look at the speck in your brother's eye and fail to notice the beam in your own? First take the beam from your own eye, and then you will see clearly to take the speck from your brother's eye. Do not give what is holy to dogs, and do not throw your pearls before swine, lest they trample them.

Ask, and it will be given to you; seek, and you will find; knock, and the door will be opened to you. Which father among you, if his son asks for bread, will give him a stone? Or if he asks for a fish, will give him a serpent? Therefore whatever you would have men do to you, do the same to them, for this sums up the Law and the Prophets."

Then He said, "Enter through the narrow gate. The gate is wide and the road broad that leads to destruction, and many go in by it. Beware of false prophets; you will know them by their fruit. Not everyone who says to Me, 'Lord, Lord,' will enter the kingdom of heaven, but the one who does My Father's will. Everyone who hears these words of Mine and acts on them is like a wise man who built his house on the rock. Rain fell, the floods came, and the winds beat against that house, but it did not fall, because it had been founded on the rock."

At another time Jesus rejoiced and said, "I thank You, Father, Lord of heaven and earth, because You have hidden these things from the wise and understanding and revealed them to little children. Yes, Father, because this was pleasing in Your sight. All things have been handed over to Me by My Father, and no one fully knows the Son except the Father, nor the Father except the Son and anyone to whom the Son chooses to reveal Him. Come to Me, all who labor and are weighed down, and I will give you rest. Take My yoke upon you and learn from Me, because I am gentle and lowly in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. My yoke is easy to bear, and My burden is light."

By the sea He taught the multitude in parables, saying, "A sower went out to sow. As he sowed, some seed fell along the path, and the birds came and devoured it. Some fell on rocky ground, where it sprang up quickly, but when the sun rose it withered because it had no depth of root. Some fell among thorns, and the thorns grew up and choked it. Some fell on good soil and brought forth fruit, some thirtyfold, some sixty, and some a hundred." Then He said, "He who has ears to hear, let him hear."

Again He said, "A lamp is not lit to be put under a basket or under a bed, but on a stand. The kingdom of God is like seed cast on the ground. A man sleeps and rises night and day, and the seed sprouts and grows, though he does not know how. The earth bears fruit by itself: first the blade, then the head, then the full grain in the head. To what shall we compare the kingdom of God? It is like a mustard seed, which is smaller than all the seeds when it is sown, yet when it has grown it becomes great. It is also like leaven, which a woman took and hid in flour until all was leavened."

To His disciples He also said, "The kingdom of heaven is like a man who sowed good seed in his field. But while men slept, his enemy came and sowed weeds among the wheat. Let both grow together until the harvest, and in the time of harvest the reapers will gather the weeds first for burning, but the wheat will be gathered into the barn. The field is the world, the good seed are the sons of the kingdom, the weeds are the sons of the evil one, and the harvest is the close of the age.

The kingdom of heaven is like treasure hidden in a field, which a man found and hid again, and in his joy he sold all he had and bought that field. Again, the kingdom is like a merchant seeking fine pearls; when he found one pearl of great worth, he went and sold all he had and bought it. Again, the kingdom is like a net cast into the sea gathering fish of every kind; when it was full, men drew it ashore and sorted the good from the bad. Therefore every scribe instructed for the kingdom is like a householder who brings out of his treasure what is new and what is old."

To the man who asked, "Who is my neighbor?" Jesus replied, "A man was going down from Jerusalem to Jericho and fell among robbers, who stripped him, wounded him, and left him half dead. A priest came that way and passed by. A Levite also saw him and passed by. But a Samaritan came to him, had compassion, bound up his wounds, set him on his own beast, and brought him to an inn to care for him." Then Jesus asked which of the three had proved neighbor to the man who fell among robbers.

When tax collectors and sinners drew near to hear Him, and others murmured, He said, "What man among you, having a hundred sheep and losing one of them, would not leave the ninety-nine and go after the one that is lost until he finds it? And when he has found it, he lays it on his shoulders rejoicing. Or what woman, having ten silver coins and losing one, would not light a lamp and search carefully until she finds it? And when she has found it, she calls together her friends and neighbors to rejoice with her."

He also said, "A man had two sons. The younger said, 'Father, give me the share of the estate that falls to me.' Not many days later he gathered everything and went into a distant country, and there he wasted his possessions in reckless living. But when he came to himself, he said, 'I will arise and go to my father.' While he was still far off, his father saw him, was moved with compassion, ran to him, embraced him, and kissed him. But the elder son was angry and would not go in, so his father came out and pleaded with him. It was fitting to rejoice, because this brother of yours was dead and lives again; he was lost and has been found."

He taught His disciples to pray and not lose heart. He said, "Suppose one of you has a friend, and goes to him at midnight, saying, 'Friend, lend me three loaves.' Even if he will not rise because of friendship, he will rise because of persistence and give what is needed. So ask, and it will be given to you; seek, and you will find; knock, and the door will be opened. Which father among you, if his son asks for bread, will give him a stone?"

He also said, "In a certain city there was a judge who did not fear God or regard man. A widow kept coming to him, saying, 'Give me justice against my adversary.' Though he was unwilling for a time, afterward he said within himself that because this widow troubles him, he would give her justice." And He also said, "Two men went up to the temple to pray." One trusted in himself that he was righteous, but the other stood far off and cried, "God, be merciful to me, a sinner." The proud man did not go down justified; the humbled man did. He also took little children in His arms and said, "Let the little children come to Me, and do not hinder them, because the kingdom of God belongs to such as these."

He warned them, "Guard yourselves from every kind of covetousness, because a man's life does not consist in the abundance of what he possesses." Then He said, "The land of a rich man produced plentifully, and he said to himself, 'I will tear down my barns and build larger ones, and there I will store all my grain and goods.' But God said to him, 'Fool, this night your soul is required of you.'" He also said, "Be dressed and ready, and keep your lamps burning. Blessed is that servant whom his master finds watching. From everyone to whom much is given, much will be required."

Again He said, "No servant can serve two masters." He also spoke of a steward who acted shrewdly because he knew an accounting was coming, and He set before them a rich man clothed in purple and fine linen while Lazarus lay poor at his gate. The rich man received good things in his lifetime, but afterward found torment, while Lazarus was comforted. So He taught them to be faithful with what passes away, because where their treasure is, there their heart will also be.

Among His disciples He said, "If anyone would be first, he must become last of all and servant of all." He set a little child in their midst and said, "Unless you turn and become like little children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven. Whoever humbles himself like this child is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven." He warned them, "Whoever causes one of these little ones who believe in Me to stumble will answer for it. If your hand or your foot causes you to stumble, cut it off; it is better to enter life maimed than to be cast away whole." He taught them, "If your brother sins, reprove him; and if he repents, forgive him." When Peter asked how often he must forgive, Jesus answered, "I do not tell you merely seven times, but again and again beyond counting."

He also answered concerning marriage, "From the beginning God made them male and female. Therefore what God has joined together, let not man separate." When a ruler asked what he must do to inherit eternal life, Jesus said, "You know the commandments." The man said, "All these I have done from my youth." Then Jesus said, "One thing is still lacking. Sell what you have, give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven; then come, follow Me." But the man went away sorrowful, because he had many possessions.

When the feasts came and He went up to Jerusalem, Jesus taught openly in the temple though His enemies watched for an opportunity against Him. He said, "My teaching is not Mine, but His who sent Me. If any man desires to do His will, he will know whether this teaching is from God. Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment." And on the great day of the feast He cried out, "If any man is thirsty, let him come to Me and drink. Whoever believes in Me, rivers of living water will flow from within him." Some said, "This is truly the Prophet." Others said, "This is the Christ." Yet many stumbled at Him because they judged by appearances.

There He also said, "I am the light of the world. Whoever follows Me will not walk in darkness, but will have the light of life." He said, "If you remain in My word, you are truly My disciples, and you will know the truth, and the truth will make you free." When they answered, "We have never been slaves to anyone," Jesus said, "Everyone who practices sin is a slave of sin. If the Son sets you free, you will be truly free." When many claimed Abraham, He said, "If you were Abraham's children, you would do Abraham's works." At the last He said, "Your father Abraham rejoiced to see My day. Before Abraham came to be, I am."

After He gave sight to the man born blind, Jesus said, "For judgment I came into this world, so that those who do not see may see, and those who think they see may become blind." Then He said, "He who enters by the door is the shepherd of the sheep. He calls his own sheep by name and leads them out." He also said, "I am the door for the sheep," and again, "I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep." At the Feast of Dedication He said, "My works bear witness to Me. My sheep hear My voice, and I know them, and they follow Me. I give them eternal life, and no one will snatch them from My hand or from My Father's hand." Some took up stones again, while others remembered John's witness and believed in Him.

In the temple courts and before hostile questioners, Jesus said, "Show Me the coin for the tax. Whose image and inscription does it bear?" When they answered, He said, "Then render to Caesar what bears Caesar's claim, and to God what belongs to God." To those who denied the resurrection He said, "The God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob is not God of the dead, but of the living." When He was asked about the greatest commandment, He answered, "The first is to love the Lord your God with all your heart, soul, mind, and strength, and the second is like it: to love your neighbor as yourself." Then He warned the people, "Beware of the scribes," for they loved honor and burdened others. He also watched a poor widow put in two small coins, and said, "This poor widow has given more than all the rest, because she gave out of her poverty all she had."

As the end drew nearer, He said, "See that no one leads you astray. Many will come in My name, and you will hear of wars and tumults, but do not be terrified. Then they will see the Son of Man coming with power and great glory. Stay awake, and pray that you may have strength to stand before the Son of Man. Watch therefore, because you do not know the day or the hour."

Then He said, "The kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and went out to meet the bridegroom. Five were wise and five were foolish." Again He said, "It is like a man going on a journey, who entrusted his possessions to his servants." The faithful servants entered their master's joy, but the fearful and slothful servant lost what had been entrusted to him. At the last He set before them the Son of Man coming in glory and all the nations gathered before Him, and He said, "He will separate them as a shepherd separates sheep from goats." To those at His right He would say, "Come, you who are blessed of My Father, inherit the kingdom prepared for you." Those who refused mercy would go away to judgment, but the righteous into life. 

# Final Journey to Jerusalem

References: Matthew 19-21; Mark 10-11; Luke 9:51-19:48; John 7-12

When the days were drawing near for Him to be taken up, Jesus set His face toward Jerusalem. He sent messengers ahead into a Samaritan village to make ready for Him, but they would not receive Him because He was going toward Jerusalem. When James and John saw it, they asked whether they should call down fire from heaven upon them, but He turned and rebuked them. So they went on to another village.

After this He appointed seventy-two others and sent them ahead by twos into every town and place where He Himself was about to come. He told them to say, "Peace to this house," to heal the sick, and to declare that the kingdom of God had drawn near. They returned with joy, saying, "Lord, even the demons submit to us in Your name." He said to them, "Do not rejoice chiefly in this, that the spirits submit to you, but rejoice that your names are written in heaven."

As they traveled, He entered a village where a woman named Martha received Him into her house. Her sister Mary sat at the Lord's feet and listened to His word, but Martha was troubled by much serving. When Martha asked that Mary be told to help her, Jesus answered, "Martha, Martha, you are anxious and troubled about many things, but one thing is needful. Mary has chosen the better part, and it will not be taken from her."

The Feast of Tabernacles was near, and His brothers urged Him to go up to Judea and show Himself openly. Jesus told them, "My time has not yet fully come." After they had gone up, He also went, not openly, but quietly. In Jerusalem there was much whispering about Him among the crowds. Some said He was good, others that He was leading the people astray, yet none spoke freely because they feared the rulers.

He went on His way through towns and villages, journeying toward Jerusalem. As He was passing between Samaria and Galilee, ten men with leprosy stood at a distance and cried out, "Jesus, Master, have mercy on us." He told them to go and show themselves to the priests, and as they went they were made clean. One of them, when he saw that he was healed, turned back, glorifying God with a loud voice, and he fell at Jesus' feet giving thanks. He was a Samaritan. Jesus said, "Were not ten cleansed? Where are the nine? Was none found to return and give glory to God except this foreigner?" Then He said to him, "Rise and go your way. Your faith has made you well."

At the Feast of Dedication Jesus was walking in the temple, in Solomon's Colonnade. The rulers surrounded Him and pressed Him to speak plainly. When they again sought to seize Him, He escaped from their hands and went away beyond the Jordan to the place where John had first been baptizing. Many came to Him there and said, "John did no sign, but all that John said about this Man was true." And many believed in Him there.

Now a certain man named Lazarus was sick, from Bethany, the village of Mary and her sister Martha. The sisters sent word to Jesus, but after hearing it He remained where He was two more days. Then He said to His disciples, "Let us go to Judea again." When He came near Bethany, Martha went out to meet Him and said, "Lord, if You had been here, my brother would not have died. Even now I know that whatever You ask from God, God will give You." Jesus said to her, "Your brother will rise again. I am the resurrection and the life. Whoever believes in Me, though he die, yet shall he live." Martha said, "Yes, Lord, I have believed that You are the Christ, the Son of God, who comes into the world."

Then Mary came to Him, fell at His feet, and said, "Lord, if You had been here, my brother would not have died." When Jesus saw her weeping, and the Jews weeping with her, He was deeply moved in spirit and troubled. He said, "Where have you laid him?" They said, "Lord, come and see." Jesus wept. Then they came to the tomb, which was a cave with a stone lying against it. Jesus said, "Take away the stone." After Martha objected because her brother had been dead four days, Jesus lifted His eyes and gave thanks to the Father. Then He cried with a loud voice, "Lazarus, come out." The man who had died came out bound hand and foot with grave cloths, and Jesus said, "Loose him and let him go."

Many of the Jews who had come to Mary believed in Him because they had seen what He did. But others went away and told the Pharisees. So the chief priests and the Pharisees gathered the council, and from that day they took counsel together to put Him to death. Therefore Jesus no longer walked openly among the Jews, but withdrew to a town called Ephraim near the wilderness and remained there with His disciples.

Again crowds gathered to Him in the region beyond the Jordan, and He taught and healed among them. Little children were brought to Him that He might touch them, but He told His disciples not to hinder them, for the kingdom of God belongs to such as these. A ruler also came running and knelt before Him, but though he went away sorrowful, Jesus continued on the road with His disciples toward Jerusalem.

As they were going up to Jerusalem, Jesus took the Twelve aside and said, "Behold, we are going up to Jerusalem, and all things written by the prophets concerning the Son of Man will be accomplished. He will be handed over, mocked, mistreated, killed, and on the third day rise." Yet they did not grasp these things. Along the way the sons of Zebedee sought places of honor, but He taught them that greatness in His kingdom belongs to the one who serves.

When He came near Jericho, blind beggars cried out, "Lord, Son of David, have mercy on us." Though many tried to silence them, they cried out all the more. Jesus stood still and called for them. When they came near, He touched their eyes and said, "Receive your sight. Your faith has made you well." Immediately they received their sight and followed Him, glorifying God.

Entering Jericho, He passed through the city, and there was a chief tax collector named Zacchaeus. Because he was small in stature, he ran ahead and climbed a sycamore tree to see Jesus. When Jesus came to the place, He looked up and said, "Zacchaeus, come down quickly, for today I must stay at your house." He hurried down and received Him joyfully. When others murmured, Jesus said, "Today salvation has come to this house, because he also is a son of Abraham. The Son of Man came to seek and to save what was lost."

Six days before the Passover Jesus came to Bethany, where Lazarus was, whom He had raised from the dead. There they made Him a supper. Martha served, and Lazarus reclined with Him. Then Mary took costly perfume, anointed Jesus, and wiped His feet with her hair, so that the house was filled with the fragrance. When Judas objected, Jesus said, "Leave her alone. She has kept this for the day of My burial. The poor you always have with you, but you do not always have Me." Because of Lazarus also, the chief priests took counsel that they might kill him as well.

The next day, when the great crowd heard that Jesus was coming to Jerusalem, He sent two disciples ahead to bring a colt on which no one had yet sat. They brought it to Him and spread garments upon it, and as He rode down from the Mount of Olives the crowds spread their cloaks and branches in the road. They cried out, "Hosanna to the Son of David! Blessed is the King who comes in the name of the Lord! Peace in heaven and glory in the highest!" Some Pharisees asked Him to rebuke His disciples, but He answered, "If these became silent, the stones themselves would cry out."

When He drew near and saw the city, He wept over it and said, "If only you had known in this day the things that make for peace. But now they are hidden from your eyes." Then He entered Jerusalem and went into the temple courts. He drove out those who sold and bought there, overturned the tables, and said, "My house shall be a house of prayer, but you have made it a den of robbers." The blind and the lame came to Him in the temple, and He healed them. The chief priests and scribes were indignant, yet they could do nothing at that hour because all the people hung upon His words.

As He went out from the city toward Bethany, He saw a fig tree in leaf but found no fruit on it, and He spoke against it. When they passed by again, the disciples saw that it had withered from the roots. Jesus taught them to have faith in God. By day He taught in the temple, and by night He went out and stayed on the mount called Olivet.

Now certain Greeks who had come up to worship at the feast sought Him through Philip and Andrew. Jesus answered, "The hour has come for the Son of Man to be glorified. Unless a grain of wheat falls into the earth and dies, it remains alone." When He prayed that the Father would glorify His name, a voice came from heaven in answer. Though He had done so many signs before them, many still did not believe openly, for they loved the praise of men more than the praise of God. After crying out once more concerning faith, light, and judgment, Jesus departed and hid Himself from them. 

# Crucifixion

References: Matthew 26-27; Mark 14-15; Luke 22-23; John 13-19

When the Passover and the Feast of Unleavened Bread were near, the chief priests and scribes were seeking a way to seize Jesus by stealth and kill Him, though they feared the people. Then Satan entered Judas Iscariot, one of the Twelve. He went away to the chief priests and captains and spoke with them about how he might deliver Jesus to them. They were glad and agreed to give him money, and from that time he looked for an opportunity to hand Him over apart from the crowd.

On the first day of Unleavened Bread, when the Passover lamb was being prepared, Jesus sent Peter and John ahead, telling them where they would find a furnished upper room made ready. They did as He said and prepared the Passover. When the hour came, He reclined at table with the apostles and said, "I have greatly desired to eat this Passover with you before I suffer."

During supper, Jesus knew that the Father had given all things into His hands, and that He had come from God and was going to God. He rose from the table, laid aside His outer garment, girded Himself with a towel, and began to wash the disciples' feet. When He came to Peter, Peter protested, but Jesus said, "If I do not wash you, you have no share with Me." After He had washed them all, He said, "I have given you an example. The servant is not greater than his master."

Then Jesus was troubled in spirit and said, "Truly I tell you, one of you will betray Me." The disciples looked at one another in grief and uncertainty, and each began to ask whether he was the one. Jesus indicated the betrayer by giving him a morsel. After Judas received it, Jesus said, "What you are going to do, do quickly." Judas went out at once, and it was night.

While they were eating, Jesus took bread, gave thanks, broke it, and gave it to them, saying, "Take and eat; this is My body, which is given for you. Do this in remembrance of Me." In the same way He took the cup after supper and gave it to them, saying, "This cup is the new covenant in My blood, poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins." He also said, "I will not again drink of the fruit of the vine until the kingdom of God comes."

A dispute also arose among them about which of them should be considered greatest. But Jesus said, "The kings of the nations rule over them; it must not be so among you. Let the greatest among you become as the youngest, and the leader as the one who serves." After Judas had gone out, He said, "A new command I give you: love one another. Just as I have loved you, so you also are to love one another. By this all men will know that you are My disciples."

Jesus said to them, "Do not let your hearts be troubled. Trust in God; trust also in Me. In My Father's house there is room enough, and I am going to prepare a place for you. If I go and prepare a place for you, I will come again and receive you to Myself, so that where I am, there you may be also. I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through Me. If you love Me, keep My commandments, and I will ask the Father, and He will give you another Helper to remain with you, the Spirit of truth. I will not leave you as orphans; I am coming to you. Peace I leave with you; My peace I give to you. Do not let your hearts be shaken, and do not be afraid."

Again He said, "I am the true vine, and My Father is the vinegrower. Remain in Me, and I in you. As the branch cannot bear fruit by itself unless it remains in the vine, so neither can you unless you remain in Me. Whoever remains in Me and I in him bears much fruit, for apart from Me you can do nothing. This is My commandment, that you love one another as I have loved you. Greater love has no one than this, that a man lay down his life for his friends." He warned them that the world would hate them as it had hated Him, yet promised that the Helper, the Spirit of truth, would bear witness concerning Him. He said, "When He comes, He will guide you into all the truth, and He will glorify Me." He also told them plainly that they would weep and lament while the world rejoiced, yet "your sorrow will be turned into joy." "In the world you will have tribulation," He said, "but take courage; I have overcome the world."

Lifting His eyes to heaven, Jesus prayed and said, "Father, the hour has come. Glorify Your Son, that the Son may glorify You. I have manifested Your name to those whom You gave Me out of the world. They were Yours, and You gave them to Me, and they have kept Your word. Keep them in Your name, that they may be one even as We are one. Sanctify them in the truth; Your word is truth. As You sent Me into the world, I also have sent them into the world. I do not ask for these only, but also for those who will believe through their word, that they all may be one, so that the world may believe that You sent Me. Father, I desire that those whom You have given Me may be with Me where I am, to see My glory."

When they had sung a hymn, they went out to the Mount of Olives. Jesus said, "All of you will stumble this night, for the shepherd will be struck and the sheep scattered." Peter answered, "Even if all others stumble, I will not." But Jesus said to him, "Truly, before the rooster crows, you will deny Me three times." Yet Peter insisted that he would die with Him rather than deny Him, and the others said the same.

Then they came to a place called Gethsemane. Jesus took Peter, James, and John farther with Him, and He began to be greatly distressed. He said, "My soul is overwhelmed with sorrow, even to death. Remain here and keep watch." Going a little farther, He fell on His face and prayed that if it were possible the hour might pass from Him; yet not His will, but the Father's, was to be done. An angel from heaven strengthened Him. He came and found the disciples sleeping, and again and again He warned them to watch and pray, that they would not enter into temptation.

While He was still speaking, Judas arrived with a crowd carrying swords and clubs from the chief priests and elders. Judas came near to greet Him with a kiss, but Jesus said, "Judas, would you betray the Son of Man with a kiss?" When Jesus stepped forward and said, "I am He," they drew back and fell to the ground. Then they laid hands on Him. Simon Peter drew a sword and struck the high priest's servant, cutting off his right ear, but Jesus rebuked him, touched the servant, and healed him. Then He yielded Himself into their hands.

They led Jesus first to Annas, and then to Caiaphas the high priest, where the scribes and elders were assembled. False witnesses came forward, yet their testimony did not agree. At last the high priest put Him under oath and asked whether He was the Christ, the Son of the Blessed One. Jesus answered, "You will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of power and coming with the clouds of heaven." Then they said He had spoken blasphemy, and they mocked Him, struck Him, and covered His face.

Peter had followed at a distance into the courtyard. There he was recognized again and again as one of Jesus' disciples. But he denied it before them all. After the third denial, immediately a rooster crowed. Then the Lord turned and looked at Peter, and Peter remembered His word. He went out and wept bitterly.

When morning came, Judas saw that Jesus had been condemned, and he was filled with remorse. He brought back the silver to the chief priests and elders, saying that he had sinned by betraying innocent blood. But they refused to take responsibility. So Judas threw the silver into the sanctuary, departed, and ended his life. The priests used the money to buy the potter's field as a burial place for strangers.

Then they led Jesus from Caiaphas to the governor's headquarters. They did not enter, so that they might remain clean for the Passover. Pilate went out to them and then brought Jesus inside for questioning. He asked, "Are You the King of the Jews?" Jesus answered, "My kingdom is not from this world. If My kingdom were from this world, My servants would have been fighting, but now My kingdom is not from here." Pilate said, "So You are a king?" Jesus answered that He had come into the world to bear witness to the truth, and that everyone of the truth hears His voice. Though Pilate found no fault worthy of death in Him, the priests pressed their accusations. Learning that Jesus was from Galilee, Pilate sent Him to Herod, who mocked Him and sent Him back arrayed in splendor.

Now at the feast it was customary to release one prisoner. The crowd, stirred up by the chief priests, asked for Barabbas, a man imprisoned for murder in the insurrection. Pilate wanted to release Jesus, but they cried out that He should be crucified. Pilate took water and washed his hands before the crowd, declaring himself innocent of this blood. Then he released Barabbas to them, but delivered Jesus to be scourged. The soldiers clothed Him in scarlet and purple, set a crown of thorns upon His head, placed a reed in His hand, struck Him, and mocked Him, saying, "Hail, King of the Jews."

Pilate brought Him out and said, "Behold the Man." But when the chief priests and officers saw Him, they cried, "Crucify Him." Pilate asked, "Shall I crucify your King?" They answered, "We have no king but Caesar." When they insisted that He had made Himself the Son of God, Pilate grew more afraid. Yet because the uproar was rising and they declared that anyone who made himself a king opposed Caesar, Pilate sat down on the judgment seat and finally handed Jesus over to be crucified. Pilate wrote a title and fixed it to the cross, naming Him Jesus of Nazareth, the King of the Jews.

As they led Him away, they seized Simon of Cyrene and laid the cross upon him to carry behind Jesus. A great multitude followed, including women who mourned for Him. But Jesus turned to them and told them not to weep for Him, but for themselves and for their children. When they came to the place called Golgotha, they offered Him wine mingled with bitterness, but He would not drink it. There they crucified Him, and with Him two others, one on either side and Jesus in the middle.

Jesus said, "Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing." The soldiers divided His garments and cast lots. Those who passed by reviled Him, and the rulers mocked Him. One of the criminals joined in the abuse, but the other rebuked him and said, "Jesus, remember me when You come into Your kingdom." Jesus answered him, "Truly I tell you, today you will be with Me in paradise." Standing near the cross were His mother, His mother's sister, Mary the wife of Clopas, Mary Magdalene, and the disciple whom He loved. Seeing His mother and that disciple, Jesus said, "Woman, behold your son." Then He said to the disciple, "Behold your mother." From that hour the disciple took her into his own home.

From about the sixth hour until the ninth, darkness came over all the land. Jesus cried out with a loud voice, "My God, My God, why have You forsaken Me?" Knowing that all had now been accomplished, He said, "I thirst." After receiving the sour wine, He cried out again, "It is finished." Then He said, "Father, into Your hands I commit My spirit," and He bowed His head and gave up His spirit. At that moment the curtain of the temple was torn, the earth shook, rocks were split, and the centurion, seeing what had happened, glorified God and confessed that truly this Man was righteous and truly the Son of God.

Because it was the day of preparation, the Jews asked that the bodies not remain on the crosses during the Sabbath. The soldiers broke the legs of the two crucified with Him, but when they came to Jesus they saw that He was already dead. Instead one of them pierced His side with a spear, and immediately blood and water came out. The crowds went away beating their breasts, while the women who had followed Him from Galilee remained at a distance, watching these things.

When evening had come, Joseph from Arimathea, a rich man and respected member of the council who was himself waiting for the kingdom of God, went boldly to Pilate and asked for the body of Jesus. Nicodemus also came, bringing a mixture of myrrh and aloes. They took the body, wrapped it in linen with the spices according to the burial custom, and laid Him in Joseph's own new tomb in a garden near the place of crucifixion. The women saw the tomb and how His body was laid, then returned and prepared spices and ointments, and on the Sabbath they rested according to the commandment. The next day the chief priests and Pharisees secured the tomb with a seal and set a guard over it. 

# Resurrection

References: Matthew 28; Mark 16; Luke 24; John 20-21

After the Sabbath, while it was still very early on the first day of the week, Mary Magdalene and the other women came to the tomb carrying the spices they had prepared. There had been a great shaking, for an angel of the Lord had come down and rolled away the stone. When they arrived, they found the stone already moved and the tomb open. Entering, they did not find the body of the Lord Jesus.

While they stood in perplexity, messengers in dazzling clothing appeared and said, "Why do you seek the living among the dead? He is not here; He has been raised. Remember how He told you while He was still in Galilee that the Son of Man must be delivered into the hands of sinful men, be crucified, and on the third day rise." Fear fell upon the women, yet they remembered His words and ran from the tomb with trembling and great joy to tell the disciples.

Mary Magdalene ran ahead and came to Simon Peter and the other disciple whom Jesus loved. She said, "They have taken the Lord out of the tomb, and we do not know where they have laid Him." Then Peter and the other disciple ran to the tomb. The other disciple arrived first and saw the linen cloths lying there. Peter went in and saw the cloths and the face cloth folded separately. Then the other disciple also went in, and he saw and believed. Yet they still did not understand from the Scripture that He had to rise from the dead. So the disciples returned to their homes.

But Mary remained outside the tomb weeping. As she stooped to look in, she saw two angels seated where Jesus' body had lain. They asked, "Woman, why are you weeping?" She answered, "They have taken away my Lord, and I do not know where they have laid Him." Turning around, she saw Jesus standing there, though she did not recognize Him. He said to her, "Woman, why are you weeping? Whom are you seeking?" Supposing Him to be the gardener, she asked where the body had been laid. Jesus said to her, "Mary." She turned and said, "Rabboni." Jesus said to her, "Do not cling to Me, for I have not yet ascended to the Father. But go to My brothers and tell them that I am ascending to My Father and your Father, to My God and your God." Mary Magdalene came and announced to the disciples, "I have seen the Lord," and told them what He had said to her.

As the other women were on their way, Jesus met them and greeted them. They came and took hold of His feet and worshiped Him. Then Jesus said, "Do not be afraid. Go and tell My brothers to go to Galilee, and there they will see Me."

Some of the guard also went into the city and reported to the chief priests all that had happened. After gathering with the elders, they gave a large sum of money to the soldiers and told them to say that His disciples came by night and stole Him away while they slept. So that story was spread widely among the Jews.

That same day two of His disciples were going to a village called Emmaus, speaking together about all that had happened. Jesus Himself came near and began walking with them, but their eyes were kept from recognizing Him. He asked what they were discussing, and they told Him of Jesus of Nazareth, mighty in deed and word before God and all the people, and of the report that some women had seen a vision of angels who said He was alive. Beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, He explained to them the things concerning Himself in all the Scriptures.

When they came near the village, He acted as though He would go farther. But they urged Him strongly to remain with them, because evening was drawing on. So He went in to stay with them. When He reclined at table, He took bread, blessed it, broke it, and gave it to them. Then their eyes were opened and they recognized Him, and He vanished from their sight. They said to one another that their hearts had been burning within them while He spoke on the road and opened the Scriptures to them. So they rose that same hour and returned to Jerusalem.

That evening, while the doors were shut for fear of the authorities and while the disciples were speaking of these things, Jesus Himself stood among them and said, "Peace to you." They were startled and thought they were seeing a spirit, but He showed them His hands, His feet, and His side. He said, "Why are you troubled? Touch Me and see, for a spirit does not have flesh and bones as you see that I have." Then He ate before them. The disciples rejoiced when they saw the Lord.

Then Jesus said to them again, "Peace to you. As the Father has sent Me, so I also send you." He breathed on them and said, "Receive the Holy Spirit." He opened their minds to understand the Scriptures and told them, "It is written that the Christ should suffer and rise from the dead on the third day, and that repentance for the forgiveness of sins should be proclaimed in His name to all nations, beginning from Jerusalem."

Thomas, one of the Twelve, was not with them when Jesus came. When the others told him that they had seen the Lord, he said, "I will not believe unless I see and touch for myself." Eight days later the disciples were again inside, and Thomas was with them. Jesus came, though the doors were shut, stood among them, and said, "Peace to you." Then He told Thomas to put away his unbelief. Thomas answered, "My Lord and my God." Jesus said to him, "Because you have seen Me, you have believed. Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed."

After this Jesus revealed Himself again to the disciples by the Sea of Tiberias. Simon Peter, Thomas, Nathanael, the sons of Zebedee, and two others had gone out to fish, but that night they caught nothing. At daybreak Jesus stood on the shore, though they did not know that it was He. He told them to cast the net on the right side of the boat, and they were no longer able to draw it in because of the multitude of fish. Then the disciple whom Jesus loved said, "It is the Lord." When they came ashore, they found a charcoal fire with fish and bread laid on it, and Jesus gave them breakfast.

After they had eaten, Jesus said to Simon Peter, "Simon son of John, do you love Me more than these?" Peter answered, "Yes, Lord, You know that I love You." Jesus said, "Feed My lambs." Again and a third time He asked him, and Peter was grieved that He asked a third time. Then Jesus told him to tend His sheep and hinted at the death by which he would glorify God. When Peter asked about the beloved disciple, Jesus told him to follow Him without comparing his path to another's.

The eleven disciples then went into Galilee, to the mountain where Jesus had directed them. When they saw Him, they worshiped Him, though some still hesitated. Jesus came and said, "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to Me. Therefore go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to keep all that I commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age."

Later He led them out as far as Bethany. Lifting up His hands, He blessed them, and while He was blessing them He was taken up from them. They worshiped Him and returned to Jerusalem with great joy, and they were continually in the temple blessing God. Jesus had done many other signs in the presence of His disciples which are not written here, but these things were written so that men might believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God, and that by believing they might have life in His name. 

# Note on Method

The narrative is arranged according to a working chronological harmonization of Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John. Where multiple Gospels report the same event, the accounts are merged into one telling rather than repeated separately.

Distinct details are preserved when they can stand together naturally. Repetition is reduced when the accounts are clearly parallel. Longer teaching material may be grouped by narrative setting or by literary coherence, depending on what best serves clarity without distorting the source texts.

Chronology decisions and merge notes were tracked during drafting so that the harmonization remained auditable through revision.

# Note on Sources

The bracketed references in the review edition identify the biblical passages informing each paragraph. They are included to help editors and careful readers verify how the narrative has been harmonized.

This prose is not a direct reproduction of any one modern English Bible translation. It is original harmonized writing intended to remain faithful to the meaning of the source texts while avoiding unnecessary dependence on the distinctive phrasing of any single translation.

For formal study, quotation, doctrinal judgment, and textual comparison, readers should return to the biblical text itself.
